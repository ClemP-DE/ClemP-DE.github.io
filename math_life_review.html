Memoir

A Mathematical Life Review

Clem Padin

You have probably heard of the term Near-Death Experience (NDE). This is an experience where, during some life threatening situation, people report that their entire life flashes before their eyes — a life review of sorts. While I didn’t have an NDE, I did experience something similar: a mathematical life review. It happened on a walk home from the local movie theater where I had just finished watching Paw Patrol with my grandsons Henry (6½) and Javi (4½). As we strolled along on that unseasonably warm January day, Henry suddenly turned to me and asked, “Do you know any math?” And in the brief moment it took me to lift my foot and take a single step, a flood of images flashed before me as I considered how to answer him. These are some of the things I saw…

  • Long Division I saw my 5th grade teacher, Mrs. Glass. She taught us long division and I loved it! I would spend hours doing exercise after exercise. There was a chalk board where she would write out the problems for us to work on in class. And I remembered the shelf that held the pieces of chalk and erasers and the small milk cartons we would buy for our break.
  • Geometry and Algebra I remembered my poor test results in 7th grade algebra — although I sailed through geometry. Eventually, I came to understand that those who do well in one of the subjects sometimes do poorly in the other. For me, a visual thinker, geometry was easy.
  • Calculus I remembered that I failed calculus in college my initial semester. Then, sometime later, there was this moment when my calculus instructor wrote down a mathematical series on the board and asked if anyone knew what it was. I was the only one to identify it as the expansion of the Sin function.
  • Trigonometry Dr. Clay, my trigonometry professor, came to mind. He taught the class from a textbook he had written, still not officially published and in paperback. He offered a dollar to any student who found an error — a challenge that caught my attention in those years when I sold my blood plasma just to afford to eat. I discovered three mistakes in his manuscript, and those three dollars meant more than just mathematical precision — they meant dinner. Years later, I returned to campus, hoping to share with him how those few dollars had touched my life during those struggling times. But I arrived too late. He had passed away just the year before, and with him went the chance to tell him how a professor’s simple gesture had meant so much to a hungry student finding his way through both mathematics and life.
  • Engineering Mathematics I recalled that day in my Engineering Math class. The professor had just completed some derivation — of what I wish I could but don’t now recall — but after he was done, I sat there stunned at how beautiful it was. All I wanted was for him to do it all again. That was, I think, the first time I realized that mathematics is a form of art and can touch you as deeply as a painting or a poem or music.
  • Topology I remembered the time when my topology professor asked if I would be his apprentice. He was moving from Arizona to Wisconsin and wanted me to help him with his work. I was flattered but I was already settled in Tucson with a house and a girlfriend who would eventually become my wife. And, with all due respect to the University of Wisconsin, I was not going to leave 300 days of clear skies, warm weather, and the other-worldliness of the Arizona desert for Wisconsin winters.
  • Differential Equations I saw myself in the university cafeteria, with the clatter of trays and murmur of voices — my favorite place to study. Like the long division days of my 10-year-old self, I loved the long derivations of differential equations. You start with a problem to be solved and then spend hours filling page after page of graceful equations. I still have those notebooks and open them from time to time and am reminded of the noise and smell of the cafeteria.
  • Thermodynamics I remembered the time I completed my thermodynamics test in 15 minutes. As I left the classroom, I heard one student whisper to another, “Doesn’t he even want to double check his answers?” I didn’t have to — it was all mathematics.
  • Letter to the Editor I recalled my letter that was published in Science News where I complained about a sculptor who fashioned equations out of marble. I said in that letter that I “tired of seeing mathematics represented in ‘time-invariant’ media” and that mathematics “seems more aligned with dance.” That comment shockingly showed up as the heading of a chapter in Clifford A. Pickover’s book The Mathematics of Oz. While I now cringe at that letter, in my defense, it goes back to long division, the Engineering Mathematics class experience, and my differential equations problem sets: that dance as your pencil moves across the page.
  • String Theory I recalled sitting in a small room at Fermilab learning about the mathematics behind a new idea called String Theory, when I should have been working in the control room monitoring the beamlines with my colleagues. But it was thrilling to see this new idea emerge.
  • Analysis I recalled waiting in line at the unemployment office reading Eight Lectures on Mathematical Analysis by A. Ya Khinchin. I had lost my job as a Fortran programmer at DuPont in Delaware, having left Fermilab just a few years earlier. I loved that book and took it everywhere. It’s worn now and falling apart but I still pick it up occasionally and suspect that one day every line in it will be underlined.
These memories and others flashed through my mind, triggered by Henry’s question: “Do you know any math?” And as my foot completed its step, I knew how to answer him. I simply said “Yes.”
