Poly-π

π is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter — and it is the same for every circle, regardless of size. Does each family of regular polygons have its own analogous constant? It does.

πn
The Idea

Every regular polygon has its own π

All circles have the same shape — they differ only in size. The same is true of equilateral triangles, squares, regular pentagons. For circles, the ratio of circumference to diameter is always π ≈ 3.14159…

By defining a polygon's radius as the distance from its centre to any vertex, and its circumference as its perimeter, a constant πn emerges for each regular n-gon: the ratio of perimeter to diameter (twice the radius) is the same for all regular n-gons of the same type.

The general formula turns out to be elegantly simple:

πn = n · sin(180° / n) As n → ∞, πn → π

As the polygon gains more and more sides and approaches a circle, its poly-π value converges to π itself.
