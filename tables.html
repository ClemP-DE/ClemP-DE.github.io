Adventures with Number Tables

A conversation with a six-year-old, a number line written on a sheet of paper, and a late-night insight about a 10×10 table and its diagonals.

+11
How It Started

A birthday walk and an unimpressed six-year-old

Walking home from a children's film on his birthday, a grandson asked whether his grandfather knew any maths. One thing led to another — a number line on a sheet of paper, the numbers 0 through 9 written out — and a challenge to circle two adjacent numbers.

The grandson was unimpressed and went off to play. But staring at the number line alone, a pattern emerged: the pairs of adjacent numbers — 01, 12, 23, 34 … 89 — all differ from their neighbours by exactly 11.

That night, drifting off to sleep, an image appeared: a 10×10 table of numbers, and those pairs sitting on a diagonal. In the morning the table was drawn out, and there they were.

The Pattern

The number line, laid out in rows of ten

When the number line is written in rows of ten, four different difference patterns emerge depending on which direction you move through the table:

Left diagonal: differ by 11 Vertical: differ by 10 Right diagonal: differ by 9 Horizontal: differ by 1
01234 56789
1011121314 1516171819
2021222324 2526272829
3031323334 3536373839
4041424344 4546474849

Yellow = left-leaning diagonal (differ by 11) · Green = vertical (differ by 10) · Blue = right-leaning diagonal (differ by 9)

None of this is deeply surprising once you see why it must be true — the paper gives a simple algebraic proof for each case. But the pattern is genuinely easy to miss until someone points it out, and once seen it opens up further questions about what happens when the row width changes.
