Conjecture

The Collatz Conjecture

Take any positive integer. If even, halve it. If odd, triple it and add one. Repeat. Will you always reach 1?

Nobody has proven this. Nobody has disproven it either. The conjecture is simple enough to explain to a child and has resisted every serious mathematical attack for nearly a century. This project explores it not through proof but through visualisation — treating the sequences it generates as a data set and using interactive tools to build intuition about what is actually happening.

The paper, submitted to the mathematics magazine Chalkdust, describes five visualisations developed through that exploration: a trajectory diagram, a sequence table, a steps histogram, an odd/even step counter, and a scatter plot of steps versus the even/odd ratio. Each one raised new questions. None of them answered the conjecture.

Geometry

Poly-π

π is the ratio of circumference to diameter — for a circle. What is the equivalent constant for a regular polygon?

All circles have the same shape. So do all equilateral triangles, all squares, all regular pentagons. For circles, the ratio of circumference to diameter is always π. This paper asks: does each family of regular polygons have its own analogous constant?

The answer is yes — and as the polygon gains more sides and approaches a circle, its constant converges to π itself. Published in Chalkdust Magazine.

Number Theory

Adventures with Number Tables

A conversation with a six-year-old, a number line, and a 10×10 table that turned out to be surprisingly interesting.

On a birthday walk home from a children's movie, a six-year-old grandson asked whether his grandfather knew any maths. The resulting conversation led to a number line on a sheet of paper — and then to a late-night insight about a 10×10 table and its diagonals.

Adjacent pairs in the number line always differ by 11. The reason turns out to be simple once you see it — and it opens up a family of related patterns depending on which direction you move through the table.

Arithmetic

Arithmetic Scavenger Hunt

Encode a secret message as arithmetic problems. Solve the sums, decode the letters, reveal the sentence.

Each letter of the alphabet corresponds to a number — A=1 through Z=26, or reversed. Type in any sentence, choose which arithmetic operations to use, and the tool generates a printable worksheet where each problem's answer decodes to a letter.

Designed to make arithmetic practice genuinely engaging — especially for children who like puzzles more than drills.

A Life and the Number Line

Ten digits, nine pairs, and an entire human life hiding in plain sight.

On a birthday, while trying to introduce a six-year-old grandson to the number line, an unexpected pattern emerged. Write out the digits 0 through 9, take each adjacent pair, and the series you get maps — almost perfectly — onto the stages of a human life.

A short reflection on what ten numbers contain.
