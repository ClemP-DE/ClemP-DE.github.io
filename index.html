Conjecture

The Collatz Conjecture

Take any positive integer. If even, halve it. If odd, triple it and add one. Repeat. Will you always reach 1?

I stumbled onto the Collatz Conjecture through a colleague who called it the “3x+1 problem.” I was immediately hooked and eventually built five interactive tools to explore it — not to prove it, but to see what the sequences actually look like when you treat them as data. Each tool raised new questions. None of them answered the conjecture. I wrote it all up for Chalkdust.

Geometry

Poly-π

π is the ratio of circumference to diameter — for a circle. What is the equivalent constant for a regular polygon?

Every circle has the same ratio of circumference to diameter — that’s π. I started wondering whether every regular polygon has its own equivalent constant. It turns out it does, and as the polygon gains more sides and gets closer to a circle, its constant converges to π itself. Published in Chalkdust Magazine.

Adventures with Number Tables

A conversation with a six-year-old, a number line, and a 10×10 table that turned out to be surprisingly interesting.

A late-night insight — I saw a 10×10 table in my mind as I was falling asleep and knew the number sequence I’d been looking at that day would land on one of its diagonals. Sure enough, it did. That led to an exploration of how the rows, columns, and diagonals of these tables behave and a few surprises along the way.

A Life and the Number Line

Ten digits, nine pairs, and an entire human life hiding in plain sight.

On my birthday I tried to show my six-year-old grandson something interesting about the number line. He was unimpressed and went off to play. But staring at those ten digits, I noticed that the adjacent pairs — 01, 12, 23… — map almost perfectly onto the stages of a human life. Ten numbers, and there it all is.
